Developer marketing is marketing to people who distrust marketing. That is
the whole problem, and every section of this guide is downstream of it.

The buyers are developers: they research continuously and independently, they
skip sales calls, they go straight to the documentation, and they weigh one
recommendation from a peer over any amount of vendor copy. Adam Frankl — who
was an early marketing executive at JFrog, Neo4j, and Sourcegraph — builds
his whole playbook on that observation: standard B2B growth tactics don't
just underperform with developers, they *backfire*, because developers read
polish as a warning label. His shortest version of the alternative: developers
don't lie to other developers, so peer validation beats vendor claims every
time.

That doesn't make developer marketing "just DevRel" or "just good docs".
There is a real marketing discipline here — positioning, messaging, channel
strategy, launches, pricing pages, paid spend — practiced by a small bench of
specialists (Jakub Czakon's Markepear is the sharpest public body of work).
It just operates under a harder constraint: everything you publish is being
evaluated by a professional skeptic with root access to your product.

## How this guide works

The guide is the evergreen layer of this site. Each section states what the
state of the art is, cites its sources, and shows a changelog of when — and
why — it changed. Two other surfaces keep it honest: **signals**, a daily
feed of what moved in the field, and **articles**, deep dives that a signal
earns when it deserves more than 300 words. When either contradicts a guide
section, the section gets fixed, and the changelog says so.

## How to read it

In order, if you're new: the sections build from audience (why developers
buy) through positioning, content, channels, and measurement. À la carte if
you're a practitioner: every section opens with its claim in the first
sentence, so you can decide in ten seconds whether it's worth your next
five minutes.
