Developers buy by evaluating, not by being persuaded — so the job of
developer marketing is to lower the cost of evaluation, not to raise the
volume of persuasion.

Everything reliable we know about the audience follows from that:

## They start at the docs, not the landing page

Lee Robinson — who helped scale Vercel to a million monthly active developers
and $100M in annual revenue before moving to Cursor — puts it bluntly:
developers skip the marketing pages and go straight to the documentation and
code samples. The docs *are* the landing page. If your quickstart is
out of date, your marketing is lying, whatever the homepage says. His
standard for announcement copy is the same discipline in miniature:
bottom-line first, scannable, exact numbers and dates, and a command the
reader can run.

## They distrust vendors and trust peers

Adam Frankl's core observation from JFrog, Neo4j, and Sourcegraph:
developers are deeply skeptical of vendor claims, and marketing built for
business executives doesn't just miss them — it actively repels them.
The corollary he builds the whole playbook on: *developers don't lie to
other developers.* A recommendation in a Slack channel, a comment thread, or
a conference hallway outweighs your best campaign. Practical consequence:
budget and effort spent making your existing users articulate and quotable
(case studies in their words, honest benchmarks they can defend) compounds;
effort spent on louder vendor claims doesn't.

## They expect to try before any conversation

The free tier isn't a growth hack, it's an admission requirement. Robinson's
reasoning: developers are skeptical of new tools and need hands-on experience
before committing — and pricing walls exclude a large share of the world's
developers besides. If hands-on requires a sales call, most of your funnel
never forms. Czakon's version of the same point: whatever your go-to-market
motion is — PLG, sales-led, open source — *devs evaluate anyway*; the motion
changes who signs the contract, not how the technical decision gets made.

## The audience is wider than it was

The AI wave changed who shows up. Helen Min and Matt Palmer's 2025 update is
the reference here: the audience now includes semi-technical builders
shipping real apps with AI assistance, and AI assistants themselves now
consume your docs alongside humans — which makes machine-readable,
example-dense documentation a distribution channel, not just a support
asset. Their sharpest line is a bar to test yourself against: "if your
quickstart takes more than a few minutes, you've already lost."

What did *not* change is the skepticism. A wider audience arrived with the
same allergy to being sold to — show over tell, features over adjectives,
frictionless first run. Treat the constants as the constitution and the
channels as implementation details.
