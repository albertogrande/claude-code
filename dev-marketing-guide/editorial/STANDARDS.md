# The house standard

This is the editorial contract for everything published on dev/market — the
guide, the signals, the articles. The skills reference this file instead of
restating it; change it here and every desk changes with it.

## The reader

One person: a sophisticated developer-marketing practitioner. They run or
advise marketing for DevTools companies, already read Markepear, Lee Robinson,
Scaling DevTools, and the DevRel newsletters, and can smell filler from the
first sentence. They are not here to be told developer marketing is important.
They are here to find out **what changed, what works, and what to do
differently** — in that order.

Earn their attention every paragraph. The test for any sentence: *would they
forward it to a colleague, or skim past it?* Cut the skimmable ones.

## Voice

- Plain, direct, confident. Write like a sharp practitioner talking to a peer
  over coffee, not like a content-marketing blog.
- Have a point of view. Neutral summaries are what the reader's RSS folder is
  for; this site exists for the judgment layered on top.
- Specific beats general, always. Name the company, quote the number, link the
  page. "Auth0 grew developer signups by shipping identity quickstarts in
  every framework" teaches; "companies should invest in developer content"
  doesn't.
- It's fine to be wrong in public; it's not fine to be vague in public. A
  falsifiable claim the reader can test beats a safe abstraction.

## Structure

- **Outcome first.** The first sentence of anything — signal, article, guide
  section — states the thing itself in plain words. No wind-up.
- **Nut graf by paragraph two.** Why this matters to the reader and what the
  piece will argue. If you can't write it, the piece isn't ready.
- Short paragraphs, working subheads (a subhead states a claim, not a topic:
  "Docs are the landing page" not "Documentation").
- End when the argument ends. No summary paragraph restating what was said.

## Anti-slop rules

Delete on sight:

- Throat-clearing openers: "In today's fast-paced world…", "Developer
  marketing is more important than ever…", "As we all know…".
- Hedging filler: "it could be argued", "somewhat", "arguably", "at the end
  of the day", "it's worth noting".
- Empty intensifiers: "game-changing", "revolutionary", "incredibly powerful",
  "truly unique".
- Symmetrical both-sides-ism when the evidence points one way.
- Lists of three where every item is a synonym for the other two.
- Any sentence that would survive unchanged in a piece about a different
  company, product, or tactic. If it's that portable, it carries no
  information.

## Verification gates

Nothing publishes without passing all of these:

1. **Every load-bearing claim traces to a fetched public source.** Load-bearing
   means: the argument changes if the claim is false. Fetch the source during
   the run — do not publish from memory or from a search-result snippet.
2. **Label the epistemic status.** Shipped is not announced; announced is not
   rumored. Say which one it is. If a claim circulates but has no primary
   source, either omit it or explicitly flag it as unverified.
3. **People and titles are current.** Roles in this field change fast
   (Lee Robinson's move from Vercel to Cursor is the canonical example).
   Re-verify a person's title before printing it.
4. **Sources are real, resolving URLs** to the original — not to an aggregator
   that mentions the original. Signals carry at least one; articles at least
   two.
5. **Numbers get a date.** Any metric, count, or price is stamped with when it
   was true.

## The take

Every signal ends with a take; every article *is* one. A good take:

- is an opinion the reader could disagree with — not a recap;
- is actionable where possible: what to do, test, steal, or stop doing;
- is one to three sentences. If it needs more, it's an article candidate.

## Editor pass

Before publishing, re-read the draft against this file, top to bottom, as a
hostile editor: strike every anti-slop violation, check every gate, and cut
10% of the words. If the piece fails a gate that can't be fixed this run,
don't publish it — a skipped day is better than a weak entry.
